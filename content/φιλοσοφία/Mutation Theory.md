**mutation theory**, idea that new [species](https://www.britannica.com/science/species-taxon) are formed from the sudden and unexpected [emergence](https://www.britannica.com/science/emergence-science) of alterations in their defining traits. Advanced at the beginning of the 20th century by Dutch botanist and geneticist [Hugo de Vries](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hugo-de-Vries) in his _Die Mutationstheorie_ (1901–03; _[The Mutation Theory](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Mutation-Theory)_), mutation theory joined two seemingly opposed traditions of evolutionary thought. First, its practitioners, often referred to as mutationists, accepted the primary [contention](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contention) of saltationist theory, which argued that new species are produced rapidly through discontinuous transformations. Saltationist theory contradicted [Darwinism](https://www.britannica.com/science/Darwinism), which held that species evolved through the gradual accumulation of [variation](https://www.britannica.com/science/variation-biology) over vast epochs. Second, mutationists tended to hold the strict Darwinian line that all differentiation is for the good of the species, which differed from the saltationist idea that some organismic variations are inherently undesirable. The second argument was [premised](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/premised) on the belief that more variation provided better opportunities for [adaptation](https://www.britannica.com/science/adaptation-biology-and-physiology) to a variable [environment](https://www.britannica.com/science/environment). The dovetailing of seemingly [antithetical](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/antithetical) traditions made mutation theory one of the vanguard movements in early 20th-century evolutionary and genetic theory.
