### Cold war 
1. The postwar 20th century was a period of intense competition between two great powers. The United States and Soviet Union engage in a relentless proliferation of nuclear arms, rapid industrialization, and advancement in space exploration (space race).
   
2. Competition was the main driver of the great technological progress during the Cold War era. The United States wouldn't have landed on the moon if they weren't competing with the USSR. Space exploration is expensive; it cost NASA billions of tax dollars. The rational incentive would not be for the interest of scientific interest but for the sake of being ahead with the competition.
   
3.  The space race was politically motivated; it was a will to dominate the Communist powers rather than an incentive of scientific discovery. After the fall of the great Soviet Empire in 1991 marked the end of the Cold War, NASA barely had any historical feat. NASA's budget peaked in the 1960s, when it consumed roughly 4% of all federal spending. Competition is an effective incentive for greatness.
   
### Human nature
> I know of the hatred and envy of your hearts. You are not great enough not to know hatred and envy. Then be great enough not to be ashamed of them! —**Thus Spoke Zarathustra (1885). Part I, Chapter 10, "On War and Warriors"**

1. Nietzsche puts forth the concept that envy and jealousy are a foundation of Greek society and a necessary condition for effective competition. Nietzsche commends the Greeks for creating a competitive behavioral system. Because they are competing with one another, people actively try to improve themselves, which benefits society as a whole. The Greeks are fostering an environment where each man is raised via his own action, leading to a stronger collective, by selfishly chasing their own objectives of glory by competing with one another.
   
2. The modern man has rendered his moral systems based on that preached lofty ideals of equality. He renders evil to everyone who stands out beyond the collective; he despises him yet has done nothing to within himself. It's psychological ressentiment—an outward, deep-seated envy of those who are above him. He makes a justifying value system to justify his own weakness, serving as a defense mechanism for spiritually inferior individuals that prevents them from overcoming their own shortcomings.
   
>    The lofty independent spirituality, the will to stand alone, and even the cogent reason, are felt to be dangers, everything that elevates the individual above the herd, and is a source of fear to the neighbour, is henceforth called EVIL, the tolerant, unassuming, self-adapting, self-equalizing disposition, the MEDIOCRITY of desires, attains to moral distinction and honour. ―**Friedrich Nietzsche, Beyond Good & Evil**

3. Envy and hatred should be directed inward. These emotions are labeled by the herd as something destructive and evil, but these powerful primordial instincts are beneficial to an effective competition; they should not be suppressed but rather redirected towards ourselves to better one's state of being. Humans operate mostly at the subconscious level; most of our thoughts and behavior are not a product of the rational mind. By making his source of envy some form of benchmark, he subconsciously stimulates himself with the will to power.
